How the Forecast Model Works

Our forecast model uses a simplified version of the economic indicators that the ECB Governing Council considers when making monetary policy decisions. The model weighs different factors based on their historical importance in ECB decision-making.

Inflation: The ECB's primary mandate is price stability, targeting inflation close to 2%. Higher inflation typically leads to rate hikes, while very low inflation may prompt rate cuts.

Economic Growth: Strong GDP growth can lead to inflationary pressures, while weak growth may justify accommodative monetary policy.

Unemployment: High unemployment suggests economic slack and may justify lower rates, while very low unemployment can signal inflationary pressures.

Core Inflation: The ECB pays special attention to core inflation (excluding volatile energy and food prices) as it better reflects underlying price trends.

Wage Growth: Strong wage growth can signal future inflationary pressures, while weak wage growth may indicate economic weakness.

Energy Prices: While volatile, energy price changes affect headline inflation and can influence short-term rate decisions.

Disclaimer: This is a simplified educational model and should not be used for investment decisions. Real ECB decisions involve many more factors and complex economic analysis. The actual ECB Governing Council considers additional factors including financial conditions, exchange rates, and forward-looking indicators.
